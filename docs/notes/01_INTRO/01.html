1 Why Networks?

Before diving into definitions, it is worth asking: why is the study of networks a distinct field at all? Most economic models treat agents as interacting through aggregate prices; a firm responds to market wages, not to the specific workers it employs. Most statistical models treat observations as i.i.d. draws. Neither framework is adequate when the identity of who you are connected to fundamentally changes outcomes.

Consider a few canonical examples:

Setting Nodes Edges Key question
Labour market Workers, firms Employment relations Why do referred workers earn more?
Epidemiology People Physical contact Who to vaccinate first?
Finance Banks Interbank loans Which bank’s failure is systemic?
Supply chain Firms Input–output flows Where does a shock propagate?
Social media Users Follows/retweets How does misinformation spread?

What all of these have in common: the structure of connections; not just their average density; determines outcomes. A disease spreading through a hub-and-spoke airline network behaves very differently from one spreading through a lattice. A worker connected to a structurally diverse set of acquaintances finds jobs faster than one embedded in a tightly clustered clique, even holding the number of connections fixed.

NoteCourse perspective

This is not primarily a machine learning course. We study networks from an economics angle: we want to model strategic behaviour, predict equilibria, and use the structure of networks to understand data on economic and social activity. Machine learning tools (GNNs, embeddings) appear later as instruments for prediction, not as the main object of study.

2 Graph Theory: The Mathematical Language of Networks

2.1 From Bridges to Graphs

The history of graph theory begins with a practical puzzle. In 1736, the city of Königsberg (now Kaliningrad) was divided by the Pregel River into four landmasses connected by seven bridges. Citizens wondered: is there a walk that crosses each bridge exactly once?

Leonhard Euler proved the answer is no, and in doing so laid the foundation for graph theory. His key insight: the problem is purely topological. The specific geography is irrelevant; what matters is only which landmasses are connected, and how many times. He observed that such a walk (now called an Eulerian path) requires every node except the start and end to have an even number of edges. Königsberg has four nodes, all with odd degree; so no such walk exists.

The abstraction Euler introduced; a set of nodes connected by edges; is exactly the graph. Every network model in this course is a specialisation of this same idea.

2.2 Formal Definitions

A graph \(G = (V, E)\) consists of:

  • \(V\): a set of vertices (nodes), \(|V| = n\)
  • \(E\): a set of edges (links), \(|E| = m\)

For an undirected, unweighted graph, edges are unordered pairs \(\{i, j\} \in E\), and the adjacency matrix is:

\[A_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \{i,j\} \in E \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

Three key properties of this matrix:

  1. Binary: \(A_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}\)
  2. Symmetric: \(A_{ij} = A_{ji}\) (since edges have no direction)
  3. Zero diagonal: \(A_{ii} = 0\) (no self-loops)

2.2.1 Variants Worth Knowing

Directed graphs: edges are ordered pairs \((i, j) \in E\), so \(A\) is no longer symmetric. Useful for citations, Twitter follows, river flows.

Weighted graphs: \(A_{ij} = w_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}\) records edge strength (trade volume, message frequency, road capacity).

Bipartite graphs: \(V\) is partitioned into two disjoint sets \(V_1, V_2\) with edges only between sets (actors ↔︎ films, buyers ↔︎ sellers, genes ↔︎ diseases).

Multigraphs: multiple edges between the same pair of nodes are allowed. We will mostly stay away from these, but they appear naturally in citation data.

3 Degree: The Simplest Node Summary

3.1 Definition and Basic Identities

Describing a network by its full adjacency matrix \(A\) is informative but unwieldy. The first summary statistic is the degree of a node: the number of edges it has.

\[k_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} A_{ij}\]

For weighted networks this generalises to strength: \(s_i = \sum_j A_{ij} w_{ij}\).

Two fundamental identities follow immediately from the definition:

Handshaking lemma: every edge contributes one to each endpoint’s degree, so the sum of degrees counts each edge twice:

\[\sum_{i=1}^{n} k_i = 2m \implies \langle k \rangle = \frac{2m}{n}\]

Density: the fraction of possible edges that actually exist:

\[\rho = \frac{m}{\binom{n}{2}} = \frac{2m}{n(n-1)} = \frac{\langle k \rangle}{n-1}\]

Real networks are almost always sparse: \(\rho \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\), meaning \(m = O(n)\) rather than \(m = O(n^2)\). This has enormous computational implications — it is what makes sparse matrix formats and igraph’s C-level operations practical.

3.2 Directed Degree

In a directed graph, a node has three degree notions:

\[k_i^{\text{out}} = \sum_j A_{ij}, \quad k_i^{\text{in}} = \sum_j A_{ji}, \quad k_i^{\text{tot}} = |\{j : A_{ij} = 1 \text{ or } A_{ji} = 1\}|\]

The conservation law: \(\sum_i k_i^{\text{out}} = \sum_i k_i^{\text{in}} = m\), so average in-degree always equals average out-degree.

In citation networks (PS1 Q4): \(k_i^{\text{out}}\) = number of papers cited by \(i\) (bibliography size); \(k_i^{\text{in}}\) = number of papers that cite \(i\) (citations received, often called “in-degree” or “citation count”).

3.3 The Degree Distribution

A single number like \(\langle k \rangle\) conceals crucial heterogeneity. The degree distribution \(p(k) = \Pr(K = k)\) gives the full picture.

Why does this matter? Consider two networks, both with \(n = 1490\) nodes and \(\langle k \rangle = 25.6\) (the 2004 political blogs network studied by Adamic & Glance). The maximum degree in that network was \(k_{\max} = 351\); more than 13 times the mean. Linear-scale histograms make this invisible. The right tool is a log–log plot of the complementary CDF:

\[\bar{F}(k) = \Pr(K \geq k)\]

If this is linear on a log–log scale, the degree distribution follows a power law: \(p(k) \propto k^{-\gamma}\). We will study why this emerges and what it implies in Lecture 2 (Section 4: Preferential Attachment).

4 Paths, Distance, and the Small World

4.1 Walks, Paths, and Geodesics

Moving from who is connected (degree) to how things flow requires thinking about paths through the network.

Formally, for an undirected graph:

  • A walk is any sequence of adjacent nodes \(i_1, i_2, \ldots, i_k\) where \(\{i_t, i_{t+1}\} \in E\) for all \(t\). Nodes and edges may repeat.
  • A path is a walk where all nodes are distinct.
  • A cycle is a closed path: \(i_1 = i_k\) with all intermediate nodes distinct.
  • The geodesic distance \(\ell_{ij}\) is the length of the shortest path between \(i\) and \(j\). If no path exists, \(\ell_{ij} = \infty\).

The diameter of a graph is the longest geodesic across all pairs: \[\text{diam}(G) = \max_{i \neq j} \ell_{ij}\] where we exclude \(\ell_{ij} = \infty\) (i.e., we measure within the largest connected component).

The mean geodesic distance (average path length) is: \[\langle \ell \rangle = \frac{1}{n(n-1)} \sum_{i \neq j} \ell_{ij}\]

4.2 Why Distance Matters: The Small World Phenomenon

In 1967, sociologist Stanley Milgram asked 296 residents of Wichita and Omaha to route a letter to a specific target in Boston; but only by passing it to someone they knew personally. Of the 64 letters that arrived, the median chain length was 5.5 intermediaries; giving rise to the phrase “six degrees of separation.”

(The study has since been critiqued: the 78% non-completion rate is not random, and the participants who succeeded were likely unusually well-connected. But the qualitative finding holds in larger digital datasets.)

A back-of-envelope argument explains why short paths are possible. Suppose each person has \(\gamma\) friends, with no overlap between social circles (a strong assumption). Then:

  • At distance 1: you can reach \(\gamma\) people
  • At distance 2: \(\gamma^2\) people
  • At distance \(d\): \(\gamma^d\) people

Setting \(\gamma^d = n\) and solving: \(d = \frac{\ln n}{\ln \gamma}\). For the US population \(n \approx 3 \times 10^8\):

\[d = \frac{\ln(3 \times 10^8)}{\ln \gamma}\]

Milgram’s result of \(d \approx 6\) implies \(\gamma \approx \exp\!\left(\frac{\ln(3\times 10^8)}{6}\right) \approx 53\) friends. That seems large; but notice Facebook mutual friends don’t count (weak ties, not acquaintances). The calculation is sensitive to the definition of “friend.”

ImportantThe key takeaway

Networks are “small” when the diameter \(D = O(\log n)\) rather than \(O(n)\). This exponential expansion along paths is what makes diseases, information, and prices propagate so quickly through real-world networks.

4.3 Computing Distances: BFS and the Facebook Networks

Breadth-first search (BFS) from a single source computes all distances in \(O(n + m)\) time. For large graphs, computing all \(\binom{n}{2}\) pairwise distances by running BFS from every node costs \(O(n(n+m))\); feasible for \(n \lesssim 10^4\), but not for the full Facebook100 dataset.

PS1, Question 1 asks you to compute diameter and mean geodesic distance for each of the 84 Facebook university networks. The key approximation: sample \(s\) random source nodes and average; the error is \(O(1/\sqrt{s})\).

Code 
## ---- Helper: sample average path length ----
sample_apl <- function(g, n_samples = 200, seed = 42) {
  set.seed(seed)
  comps <- components(g)
  lcc   <- induced_subgraph(g, which(comps$membership == which.max(comps$csize)))

  n_lcc   <- vcount(lcc)
  sources <- sample(seq_len(n_lcc), min(n_samples, n_lcc))

  total <- 0; count <- 0
  for (src in sources) {
    dists <- distances(lcc, v = src, mode = "out")
    finite_d <- dists[dists > 0 & is.finite(dists)]
    total <- total + sum(finite_d)
    count <- count + length(finite_d)
  }
  if (count == 0) return(NA_real_)
  total / count
}

## ---- Helper: double-sweep diameter lower bound ----
approx_diameter <- function(g) {
  comps <- components(g)
  lcc   <- induced_subgraph(g, which(comps$membership == which.max(comps$csize)))

  # Step 1: BFS from node 1
  d1 <- distances(lcc, v = 1)
  v  <- which.max(d1)

  # Step 2: BFS from v
  d2 <- distances(lcc, v = v)
  max(d2[is.finite(d2)])
}

5 Centrality: Who Matters, and Why?

5.1 Degree Centrality Is Not Enough

Degree captures local connectivity; how many direct neighbours a node has. But in many settings, influence is determined by position in the network structure, not just by raw connectivity.

Consider a node with degree 5 sitting at the junction between two otherwise disconnected communities, versus a node with degree 10 embedded in a tightly knit clique. The former is far more influential for information flow, despite lower degree.

This motivates centrality measures that capture global structural importance.

5.2 Betweenness Centrality

The betweenness centrality of node \(k\) counts how often \(k\) lies on the shortest path between other pairs of nodes:

\[B_k = \frac{1}{\binom{n-1}{2}} \sum_{\substack{i \neq j \\ k \notin \{i,j\}}} \frac{P_k(i,j)}{P(i,j)}\]

where \(P(i,j)\) is the number of geodesic paths between \(i\) and \(j\), and \(P_k(i,j)\) is the number that pass through \(k\). The normalisation by \(\binom{n-1}{2}\) makes \(B_k \in [0, 1]\).

Interpretation: a node with high betweenness is a broker; it controls traffic between otherwise separate parts of the network. Removing such nodes tends to dramatically fragment the graph.

5.2.1 The Medici Example

15th-century Florence witnessed the rise of the Medici family from relative obscurity to political and economic dominance. The puzzle: many other families were wealthier and had longer aristocratic pedigrees.

Padgett & Ansell (1993) digitised the marriage network of Florentine families and computed betweenness centralities. The Medici had \(B_{\text{Medici}} = 0.522\) — the highest of any family, by a wide margin. The Medici were positioned at the structural centre of the marriage network, serving as the broker connecting families that could not directly influence each other. Their political rise was not incidental: it was written into the topology of the network.

TipBetweenness as an economic asset

High betweenness creates information brokerage: brokers hear news, prices, and opportunities before they diffuse to the rest of the network. It also creates structural holes (Burt 1992): the broker can play parties off against each other when they cannot coordinate directly.

5.3 Closeness Centrality

Closeness centrality measures how quickly a node can reach all others:

\[C_k = \frac{n-1}{\sum_{j \neq k} \ell_{kj}}\]

A node with high closeness sits near the “centre of gravity” of the network and can spread information to all others quickly. Unlike betweenness, it does not depend on being a bottleneck; it rewards being well-connected on average.

5.4 Eigenvector Centrality and PageRank

Both betweenness and closeness treat all paths equally. But a link from a high-status node should count more than a link from a peripheral one. This motivates eigenvector centrality:

\[x_i = \frac{1}{\lambda} \sum_j A_{ij} x_j \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad Ax = \lambda x\]

The centrality vector \(x\) is the leading eigenvector of \(A\) (by the Perron–Frobenius theorem, this has all non-negative entries for connected graphs). Each node’s score is proportional to the sum of its neighbours’ scores.

PageRank (Brin & Page, 1998) adds a damping factor to handle dangling nodes and make the walk ergodic:

\[r_i = \frac{1-d}{n} + d \sum_j A_{ji} \frac{r_j}{k_j^{\text{out}}}\]

With damping \(d = 0.85\): with probability \(d\) a random walker follows an outgoing link; with probability \(1-d\) it teleports to a random node. PageRank is the stationary distribution of this random walk.

6 Clustering: Triadic Closure and Community Structure

6.1 Motivation: Triangles Are Everywhere

Degree and betweenness describe individual nodes. A different question asks about the local structure around a node: to what extent are my neighbours also connected to each other?

This matters because:

  1. Trust propagation: if A trusts B and A trusts C, there is social pressure for B and C to also form a relationship; what Simmel called the principle of triadic closure.
  2. Community detection: clusters in networks are regions of high local clustering separated by sparse cross-community edges.
  3. Model validation: the Erdős–Rényi model predicts \(C \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\), but real networks maintain high clustering. This falsifies ER as a realistic model.

6.2 Global Clustering (Transitivity)

The global clustering coefficient (or transitivity) is the fraction of connected triples that are closed into triangles:

\[C(A) = \frac{\text{number of triangles} \times 3}{\text{number of connected triples}} = \frac{\sum_{ijk} A_{ij} A_{jk} A_{ki}}{\sum_{ijk} A_{ij} A_{jk}}\]

The factor of 3 accounts for the three different “pivot” nodes in each triangle (each triangle contributes to three distinct connected triples). This makes \(C \in [0, 1]\).

In matrix terms, the numerator is \(\text{tr}(A^3)\) (each walk \(i \to j \to k \to i\) of length 3 is a triangle), and the denominator is \(\sum_{i} k_i(k_i - 1)\).

6.3 Local Clustering Coefficient

The local clustering coefficient for node \(i\) with degree \(k_i \geq 2\) is:

\[C_i(A) = \frac{\text{edges among neighbours of } i}{\binom{k_i}{2}} = \frac{2 L_i}{k_i(k_i - 1)}\]

where \(L_i\) is the number of edges among \(i\)’s \(k_i\) neighbours.

The average clustering coefficient \(\bar{C} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_i C_i\) weights all nodes equally regardless of degree, giving more weight to low-degree nodes compared to the global \(C(A)\).

Note\(C\) vs \(\bar{C}\): which to use?

\(C(A)\) (global transitivity) is the right metric when you care about the fraction of triangles in the graph as a whole. \(\bar{C}\) is more sensitive to the local structure around typical low-degree nodes. They can differ substantially: hubs with high degree contribute little to \(\bar{C}\) (since their many neighbours are unlikely to all be connected) but dominate \(C(A)\).

clustering_report <- function(g, name) {
  c_global <- transitivity(g, type = "global")
  c_avg    <- transitivity(g, type = "average")   # avg local CC, isolates excluded
  deg      <- degree(g)
  cc_local <- transitivity(g, type = "local")

  valid <- which(deg >= 2 & !is.nan(cc_local))
  corr  <- cor(deg[valid], cc_local[valid])

  cat(sprintf("%s:\n", name))
  cat(sprintf("  Global transitivity C(A) = %.4f\n", c_global))
  cat(sprintf("  Avg local CC  C_bar      = %.4f\n", c_avg))
  cat(sprintf("  Corr(degree, local CC)   = %.4f%s\n",
              corr,
              ifelse(corr < 0, "  (hubs less clustered)", "")))
}

7 The Strength of Weak Ties

7.1 Granovetter’s Paradox

One of the most celebrated findings in sociology (Granovetter 1973, the most cited paper in the sociology of networks) concerns an apparent paradox in job referrals:

Workers in the labour market find jobs primarily through referrals from acquaintances; not from close friends.

Why would weaker connections be more useful? The answer lies in the topology of clustering.

7.2 Triadic Closure and Local Bridges

Define a strong tie as a close friendship and a weak tie as an acquaintance. The Strong Triadic Closure (STC) property says: if node \(A\) has strong ties to both \(B\) and \(C\), then there should be at least a weak tie between \(B\) and \(C\).

Intuitively: if \(A\) is close friends with both \(B\) and \(C\), it is socially costly and informationally inconsistent for \(B\) and \(C\) to not even know each other.

A local bridge between \(A\) and \(B\) is an edge whose removal would increase \(\ell_{AB}\) substantially (say, to \(> 2\)). Local bridges connect otherwise distant parts of the network.

Claim: if STC holds and \(A\) has at least two strong ties, then any local bridge involving \(A\) must be a weak tie.

Proof sketch: suppose \(A\)–\(B\) is a strong tie and a local bridge. By STC, for every other strong neighbour \(C\) of \(A\), there must be an edge \(B\)–\(C\). But then removing \(A\)–\(B\) would not increase \(\ell_{BC}\) much (path goes through \(A\)–\(C\)–… to \(B\)), contradicting “local bridge.” Contradiction. \(\square\)

Implication for job referrals: close friends move in the same social circles. They know the same people, the same managers, the same opportunities. An acquaintance (weak tie) is embedded in a different cluster and therefore carries non-redundant information about job opportunities you haven’t heard of.

7.3 LinkedIn RCT Validation

Rajkumar et al. (2022) ran a massive randomised controlled trial: over 2015–2019, LinkedIn adjusted its “People You May Know” algorithm for 20+ million users. Some users received more weak-tie suggestions, others more strong-tie suggestions.

Key results: - New connections formed from weak-tie suggestions led to more job transmissions than those from strong-tie suggestions - But the relationship is an inverted U: moderately weak ties (around 10 mutual connections) were optimal - Ties with 0 mutual connections (pure strangers) were not useful either - The effect was strongest in high-technology sectors

This is a rare case where a classic sociological theory was validated at scale using a pre-registered experiment with billions of data points.

8 Networks in Economics: Price of Anarchy

The final section of the lecture places networks in an economic context. Networks are not passive structures; agents make strategic decisions on them. This introduces game-theoretic considerations.

8.1 Braess’s Paradox

Consider a road network where one unit of traffic must travel from \(s\) to \(t\). Two routes exist:

  • Route 1: \(s \xrightarrow{x_1} A \xrightarrow{1} t\), cost \(= x_1 + 1\)
  • Route 2: \(s \xrightarrow{1} B \xrightarrow{x_2} t\), cost \(= 1 + x_2\)

where \(x_1, x_2\) are flows on the congested links (cost = flow on those links).

At the social optimum: split traffic equally. \(x_1 = x_2 = 1/2\). Total cost \(= \frac{3}{2}\).

At the Nash equilibrium: each infinitesimal driver chooses the route with lowest cost given others’ choices. Equilibrium also has \(x_1 = x_2 = 1/2\) and total cost \(= \frac{3}{2}\).

Now add a zero-cost road from \(A\) to \(B\). Three routes now exist, including \(s \to A \to B \to t\) with cost \(x_1 + 0 + x_2\). At the new Nash equilibrium: everyone takes the \(s \to A \to B \to t\) route, giving \(x_1 = x_2 = 1\) and cost \(= 2 > \frac{3}{2}\).

Adding a road made everyone worse off. This is Braess’s paradox (1968), and it has been observed empirically in Seoul, Stuttgart, and New York.

The Price of Anarchy quantifies the efficiency loss:

\[\text{PoA} = \frac{C(\text{Nash})}{C(\text{Social Optimum})}\]

For the Braess example: \(\text{PoA} = 2 / (3/2) = 4/3\).

NoteWhy this matters

Braess’s paradox shows that network structure interacts with strategic behaviour in non-obvious ways. Infrastructure investment, drug trafficking crackdowns (Dell 2015), and platform design all face versions of this problem. Understanding when adding capacity helps versus hurts requires understanding the network topology.

9.2 The Critical Gap: Where Do These Numbers Come From?

We can compute all of these measures on any real network. But then what?

Suppose we find that the American75 Facebook network has \(C = 0.15\). Is that high? Low? Expected? To answer this, we need a null model; a probability distribution over graphs with the same basic parameters (\(n\), \(\langle k \rangle\)) against which to compare the observed statistic.

This is the core motivation for random graph models, the subject of Lecture 2.

Key questions that random graphs will let us answer:

  1. What clustering coefficient should we expect from a network with \(n\) nodes and mean degree \(\langle k \rangle\)? (Erdős–Rényi model → answer: \(C \approx 0\))
  2. Why does clustering in real networks vastly exceed the ER prediction? (Watts–Strogatz small-world model)
  3. Why do real networks have such heavy-tailed degree distributions? (Barabási–Albert preferential attachment)
  4. Can we infer latent community structure from the observed edge pattern? (Stochastic Block Model)
